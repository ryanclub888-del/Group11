Why use AI?

Effective:

AI improves fraud detection by identifying complex and evolving patterns that traditional systems and human analysts may overlook.

Efficient:

AI processes massive volumes of data in real time, allowing fraud to be detected with greater speed, accuracy, and scale.

Adaptive:

AI continues to learn from new fraud tactics, making it a strong long-term solution for protecting both lenders and borrowers.

What are the core techniques

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

GNNs improve fraud detection by analyzing the hidden relationships between connected data points, rather than examining each entity separately. This makes them especially effective at detecting synthetic identity fraud, where real and fake information is combined to appear legitimate.

Practical case:

  • NVIDIA reported up to 40% improvement in fraud detection accuracy when combining GNNs with traditional models

  • AWS helped its large e-commerce sports gadgets customer use GNNs to uncover 10 times more fraudulent accounts than previous models

  • Global banks piloting GNNs have noted better results in credit card fraud detection, more accurate anti-money laundering alerts, and faster investigations

Anomaly detection algorithms

Anomaly detection algorithms identify unusual patterns by learning what normal borrower behavior looks like and flagging activity that deviates from it. This helps detect fraud early, especially synthetic identities, by spotting subtle irregularities in applications, repayment behavior, and device or location usage.

  • Speed checking: If a large number of applications come from the same device or IP address within a short period, it would be flagged as an anomaly. Fraudulent groups often use the same infrastructure to submit multiple fake applications.

  • Anomaly Behavior: Fake identities establish credit differently from real users. Real users' early credit records are often chaotic and inconsistent, while fake identities exhibit unusually "perfect" behavior, always making timely payments and deviating from preset patterns.

  • Group Analysis: The algorithm compares new customers' behavior with their own historical records and with the group of real customers who opened accounts at the same time.
